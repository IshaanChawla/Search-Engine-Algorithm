		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">What killed the white dwarfs? (Aside from the giant explosion)</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Merger or extra matter? Two papers come to opposite conclusions.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by     <a itemprop="url" href="http://arstechnica.com/author/matthew-francis/" rel="author"><span itemprop="name">Matthew Francis</span></a>
      -  <span class="date" data-time="1432141244">May 20, 2015 5:00 pm UTC</span>
  </p>

			<div class="mobile-meta-controls">

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    
<figure class="intro-image image center full-width" style="width:640px">
      <img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/type-Ia-illustration-640x427.jpg" width="640" height="427">
  
    <figcaption class="caption">
			<div class="caption-text">Artist's impression of a pair of white dwarfs, spiraling in toward each other. Such a system could be the progenitor of a type Ia supernova.</div>
	
			<div class="caption-credit">
							<a rel="nofollow" href="https://www.cfa.harvard.edu/news/su201117">Tod Strohmayer (GSFC), CXC, NASA, Illustration: Dana Berry (CXC)</a>
				</div>
	  </figcaption>
  </figure>




    <p>Type Ia supernovae are explosions that occur when white dwarfs strip matter off a companion star, exceed their maximum possible mass, and blow up.</p>
<p>No, wait: type Ia supernovae are the explosions caused when two white dwarfs collide.</p>
<p>While it's reasonably certain that white dwarfs—the Earth-size remnant of stars similar to the Sun—are involved, the observational evidence for how these supernovae actually explode is messy. This week's issue of <em>Nature</em> is a prime example: two back-to-back papers provide evidence for a white dwarf-companion star explosion and a two-white-dwarf collision scenario, respectively. Ultimately, these apparently contradictory results could mean there are two distinct types of white dwarf supernovae... or that we still don't understand what's going on.</p>
<p>The stakes are high. Unlike other supernovae, which involve the death of a star much more massive than the Sun, type Ia supernovae all explode in very similar ways. The pattern of light they emit during and after the explosion provides a reliable measurement of how far away they are. Since supernovae are bright enough to be visible from billions of light-years away, astronomers use them to measure the expansion and acceleration rate of the Universe, as recognized in the <a href="http://arstechnica.com/science/2011/10/physics-nobel-goes-to-the-dark-side-of-the-universe/">2011 Nobel Prize in physics</a>. Because they are so important to cosmology, researchers want to understand what objects are involved in the explosion and exactly how they blow up.</p>
<aside class="pullbox sidebar right" style="width: 300px"><h2 class="subheading">Why do type Ia supernovae have such a dull name?</h2>The bland name "type Ia" comes from the spectrum of light these supernovae make. Supernovae of type I (that's the Roman numeral) don't contain much hydrogen, while type II explosions do. Type Ia is the subtype with more light emitted at the blue end of the spectrum than the red, while other type I supernovae are more balanced, color-wise. The lack of hydrogen and the way the light varies as the supernova fades led astronomers to conclude type Ia supernovae are exploding white dwarfs.</aside>
<p>The first <em>Nature</em> paper, by Yi Cao and colleagues, presents observational data on strong ultraviolet emissions from a type Ia supernova in a galaxy roughly 306 million light-years away. The light from this supernova reached Earth between May 2 and May 3, 2014, where instruments aboard the orbiting Swift observatory detected it. This is the first time a strong ultraviolet pulse of this sort has been detected, and the authors of the paper argue it originates from the shock wave of matter from an exploding white dwarf hitting its companion star.</p>
<p>By contrast, in the second <em>Nature</em> paper, Rob P. Olling and coauthors examine three explosions detected by the Kepler observatory, and they find no sign of a companion star. While Kepler was designed to monitor stars for signs of exoplanets, it was aimed at a single patch of the sky for years on end, making it capable of detecting transient events that might be missed by other telescopes. (Kepler now operates as the <a href="http://arstechnica.com/science/2014/12/nasas-kepler-back-in-the-planet-hunting-game/">K2 mission</a>; thanks to failed reaction wheels, it cannot point in a single direction any more.) The authors of the <em>Nature</em> paper could not find the expected signature of the material ejected by the explosion interacting with a companion star in any of them.</p>
<p>So why the contradiction?</p>
<p>Astronomers agree that <a href="http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v512/n7515/full/nature13672.html">white dwarfs are involved in type Ia supernovae</a>. That's because of the nature of the light spectrum they produce. When a star like the Sun runs out of hydrogen and helium fuel in its core, it isn't massive enough to keep nuclear fusion going. Instead, the dying star sheds its outer layers, exposing the core, which is made mostly of carbon and oxygen. The core is what we know of as a white dwarf, though the name "white" is a bit misleading, as they can be a range of colors.</p>
<p>However, there's another way white dwarfs can be born: if a star like the Sun loses its envelope of gas rather than shedding it, the naked core—which could have different chemical compositions—is also a white dwarf.</p>
<p>Theoretically, there's a maximum mass the white dwarf can have before it becomes unstable: the Chandrasekhar limit, roughly 1.4 times the mass of the Sun. That's because the white dwarf is kept from gravitational collapse by a quantum mechanical force known as degeneracy pressure (derived from the Pauli exclusion principle). If something adds mass to the object, degeneracy is no longer enough, and the white dwarf collapses, sending out violent shock waves and creating a supernova.</p>
<p>Which brings us back to the two new <em>Nature</em> papers and the question of how these explosions occur. In the "single-degenerate" scenario, a white dwarf (the "degenerate" object) very near the Chandrasekhar limit is locked in mutual orbit with another star. The white dwarf strips material off its companion—a process we've observed in <a href="https://astronomy.swin.edu.au/cms/astro/cosmos/R/Recurrent+Novae">other systems</a>—until it has gained enough mass to blow up.</p>
<p>In the "double-degenerate" scenario, two white dwarfs are all that remains of a pair of stars that have lost their outer layers. Neither individual white dwarf is necessarily close to the Chandrasekhar mass, but if they collide, their combined mass will be bigger than the stable limit, and the result again is a type Ia supernova.</p>

<p>The problem is that to distinguish between these possibilities, astronomers must be able to see the explosion in some detail as it is happening. If the white dwarf has a companion, that star would absorb some of the light emitted by the supernova, causing additional shock waves and light emission.</p>
<p>Because supernovae are rare in any given galaxy and most galaxies are too far away to pick out individual star systems (much less tiny white dwarfs), researchers either have to practice eternal vigilance or be very lucky. The Swift observatory caught an ultraviole(n)t burst of light, which may have come from a companion star, because it is designed to catch transient events known as gamma ray bursts. The Kepler observatory didn't see anything like that despite monitoring the explosions of three other white dwarfs.</p>
<p>The obvious solution may have occurred to you: maybe type Ia supernovae don't all explode the same way. Many previous observations of white dwarf supernova remnants have failed to find detritus from a companion star. Similarly, only a small fraction of white dwarfs may have masses near the Chandrasekhar limit, which would naturally lead to fewer single-degenerate explosions. However, there are a few cases where astronomers <em>have</em> found the remnants of companion stars. As Cao and colleagues note, an ultraviolet observatory dedicated to hunting for transient events could help resolve the question of how many explosions involve a companion compared to double-degenerate systems... or if there's still something weird going on.</p>
<p><em>Nature</em>, 2015. DOI: <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/nature14440">10.1038/nature14440</a> and <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/nature14455">10.1038/nature14455</a>  (<a href="http://arstechnica.com/science/news/2010/03/dois-and-their-discontents-1.ars">About DOIs</a>).</p>
