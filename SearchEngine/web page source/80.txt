		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">Rhetoric flies as deadline looms to renew bulk phone metadata surveillance</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Spying program Snowden exposed expires June 1 unless reauthorized by Congress.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">

		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">

    <p>"This has been a very important part of our effort to defend the homeland since 9/11. We know that the terrorists overseas are trying to recruit people in our country to commit atrocities in our country."</p>
<p>Those were the words of Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell. The GOP leader was rallying behind his legislation that would prevent the June 1 expiration of the phone metadata spying program Edward Snowden exposed two years ago.</p>
<p>McConnell's statement Sunday on ABC's <em>This Week</em> comes days after the House <a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2015/05/house-votes-338-88-to-stop-bulk-phone-surveillance/">passed a measure</a> known as the USA Freedom Act. Supported by the President Barack Obama administration, the measure would dramatically revise the surveillance program—the first time following the 9/11 terror attacks that lawmakers have voted to reduce the surveillance state.</p>
<p>But was McConnell exaggerating?</p>
<p>Yes. Well, that is if you believe the Obama-commissioned Privacy and Civil Liberties Board. Last year it concluded that the program <a href="http://www.wired.com/2014/01/watchdog-phone-spying-illegal/">didn't help the nation's spies counter terrorism</a>.</p>
<p>"Based on information provided to the Board, we have not identified a single instance involving a threat to the United States in which the telephone records program made a concrete difference in the outcome of a counterterrorism investigation," the board concluded. "Moreover, we are aware of no instance in which the program directly contributed to the discovery of a previously unknown terrorist plot or the disruption of a terrorist attack."</p>
<p>Whatever the truth, a survey released Monday of 1,001 likely voters suggested that 60 percent of Americans <a href="https://www.aclu.org/sites/default/files/field_document/privacy_poll_results.pdf">want surveillance reform</a> while 34 percent want to preserve the status quo.</p>
<p>A Senate vote on the House's USA Freedom Act, meanwhile, could come later this week if McConnell allows it to the floor. McConnell wants lawmakers to approve his bill that would allow the program to continue unabated, as is, until 2020. And as the deadline looms, he'll settle for a <a href="http://www.politico.com/story/2015/05/patriot-act-spying-programs-117976.html?hp=t3_r">two-month extension</a> of the spying program to buy time to work out a deal.</p>
<p>"I don’t want us to go dark, in effect, and I’m afraid that the House-passed bill will basically be the end of the program, and we’ll not be able to have yet another tool that we need to combat this terrorist threat from overseas," the Kentucky lawmaker said.</p>
<p>Some lawmakers, like Oregon Senator Ron Wyden, a Democrat from Oregon, and Senator Rand Paul, a Republican from Kentucky, <a href="http://www.unionleader.com/article/20150511/NEWS0605/150519880">said</a> they might filibuster a vote to extend Section 215 of the Patriot, the law in question.</p>
<p>"I'm tired of extending a bad law," Wyden <a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2015/05/wyden-if-senate-tries-to-renew-nsa-spying-authority-ill-filibuster/">said</a>. "If they come back with that effort to basically extend this for a short-term without major reforms like ending the collection of phone records, I do intend to filibuster."</p>
<p>Still, even the reform measure that Wyden and Paul support is giving privacy advocates pause.</p>
<p>Under that measure, instead of the NSA collecting and housing the metadata from every phone call made to and from the United States, that same data would remain with phone companies. The measure demands the NSA to get clearance from the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court before requiring that the telecoms hand over metadata based on an NSA search query.</p>
<p>The Fourth Amendment standard of "probable-cause" is not a necessary element to demand data from the telcos. The government says it only queries the metadata if it has "reasonable," suspicion about a terrorism target.</p>
<p>"This bill purports to ban certain acts under narrow authorities—but it doesn't ban those behaviors outright, nor does it increase meaningful oversight of the NSA. If there's one thing we know about the NSA, it's that it will stretch the law to its limits," <a href="http://demand-progress.tumblr.com/post/117623640615/demand-progress-and-credo-mobile-blast-usa-freedom">said</a> David Segal, executive director of Demand Progress. "If this bill passes, the NSA will continue unaddressed surveillance programs and will secretly torture the English language to devise novel justifications for spying on Americans—we won’t even know the details until a new whistleblower comes forward a decade or two from now."</p>
<p>The back-and-forth banter comes amid the backdrop of a May 7 federal appeals <a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2015/05/phone-metadata-spying-not-authorized-by-patriot-act-appeals-court-says/">court decision</a> that the spying program Snowden exposed "exceeds the scope of what Congress has authorized in the Patriot Act."</p>
<p><span style="line-height: 1.5">Former NSA general counsel Stewart Baker, however, has argued that the ruling by the 2nd US Circuit Court of Appeals amounts to a "97-page law review article."</span></p>
<p>"Its significance is close to zero," Baker <a href="http://www.nationaljournal.com/tech/patriot-act-in-uncharted-legal-territory-as-deadline-approaches-20150510">said</a>.</p>
