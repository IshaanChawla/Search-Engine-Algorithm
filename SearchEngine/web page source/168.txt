		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">FBI: researcher admitted to hacking plane in-flight, causing it to “climb”</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Chris Roberts "overwrote code" on Thrust Management Computer, according to affidavit.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">

  </p>

    <p>A <a href="https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/2082796-gov-uscourts-nynd-102002-1-0.html">newly-published search warrant</a> application shows that an aviation computer security researcher told the FBI that he briefly took control of at least one commercial airliner. The warrant, which was filed in a federal court in New York state, was first <a href="http://aptn.ca/news/2015/05/15/hacker-told-f-b-made-plane-fly-sideways-cracking-entertainment-system/">published</a> Friday by APTN, a Canadian news site.</p>
<p>According to the <a href="https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/2082796-gov-uscourts-nynd-102002-1-0.html#document/p13/a217963">affidavit for the warrant application</a>, the researcher, Chris Roberts, told the FBI that he:</p>
<blockquote><p>connected to other systems on the airplane network after he exploited/gained access to, or "hacked" the [in-flight entertainment] system. He stated that he then overwrote code on the airplane’s Thrust Management Computer while aboard a flight. He stated that he successfully commanded the system he had accessed to issue the climb command. He stated that he thereby caused one of the airplane engines to climb resulting in a lateral or sideways movement of the plane during one of these flights. He also stated that he used Vortex software after compromising/exploiting or "hacking" the airplane’s networks. He used the software to monitor traffic from the cockpit system.</p></blockquote>
<p>Roberts did not immediately respond to Ars’ request for comment, but he told <em><a href="http://www.wired.com/2015/05/feds-say-banned-researcher-commandeered-plane/?mbid=social_twitter">Wired</a></em> on Friday that this paragraph was taken out of context.</p>
<p>"It would appear from what I’ve seen that the federal guys took one paragraph out of a lot of discussions and a lot of meetings and notes and just chose that one as opposed to plenty of others," he said, declining to elaborate further.</p>
<p>As Ars previously <a href="http://arstechnica.com/security/2015/04/researcher-who-joked-about-hacking-a-jet-plane-barred-from-united-flight/">reported</a>, Roberts was detained and questioned by the FBI in April 2015 after he landed on a United Airlines flight from Denver, Colorado to Syracuse, New York.</p>
<p>While on board that flight, he tweeted a joke about taking control of the plane's<a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine-indicating_and_crew-alerting_system"> engine-indicating and crew-alerting system</a>, which provides flight crews with information in real-time about an aircraft's functions  including temperatures of various equipment, fuel flow and quantity, and oil pressure. In <a href="https://twitter.com/Sidragon1/status/588433855184375808">the tweet</a>, Roberts jested: "Find myself on a 737/800, lets see Box-IFE-ICE-SATCOM, ? Shall we start playing with EICAS messages? 'PASS OXYGEN ON' Anyone ? :)"</p>
<p>FBI agents then questioned Roberts for four hours and confiscated his iPad, MacBook Pro, and storage devices.</p>
<p>Since this incident, United has instituted a <a href="https://www.united.com/web/en-US/content/contact/bugbounty.aspx">bug bounty program</a>.</p>
<p>After this revelation, Roberts was roundly criticized by his professional peers on Twitter.</p>
<blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
<p dir="ltr" lang="en">There's really not much any of you can do to convince me that sending low level commands to an engine in flight is anywhere near safe.</p>
<p>— Wesley McGrew (@McGrewSecurity) <a href="https://twitter.com/McGrewSecurity/status/599389213214187520">May 16, 2015</a></p></blockquote>
<blockquote class="twitter-tweet" lang="en">
<p dir="ltr" lang="en">Either he lied about fucking with live planes or actually fucked with live planes. Both are unacceptable for an infosec professional.</p>
<p>— Nick DePetrillo (@nickdepetrillo) <a href="https://twitter.com/nickdepetrillo/status/599385466824204288">May 16, 2015</a></p></blockquote>
<p>Roberts has not been arrested, nor charged with a crime.</p>
