That model has the built-to-a-price corporate-spec TN panel that, at least, isn't 1366x768. The IPS panels really make a big difference.<br /><br />ETA: In fact, skip this one entirely and get either the X1 Carbon (if you want an ultrabook and like Lenovo's design language) or the prior-gen T440p, which is actually expandable and doesn't use soldered-down RAM.</div><br /><br />Everything's a compromise. With the X1 Carbon, you get a slightly worse keyboard with a decidedly worse layout--and since I do a lot of typing, that was a critical factor.  With the T440p, you get no physical buttons on the touchpad, which really rules out the whole T440 line from any consideration whatsoever.<br /><br />Yeah, not everything is perfect in T450s-land, either, but the problems are more minor IMO.  The keyboard still doesn't have quite a standard layout, but it's close enough.  One of the RAM modules is soldered, but I'm not likely to go over the system limit within the laptop's lifetime.  I've also seen these with both the TN and IPS panel, and while the IPS panel is better, the TN panel may be adequate or even preferred for some users.  For normal work (not photo or video work, where the colors matter more), the better color fidelity, extra brightness, and off-axis viewing of the IPS panel are irrelevant--in fact the TN panel is too bright at maximum brightness, and needs to be taken down a notch.  And if you have a lot of older Windows apps that don't handle DPI scaling well, the TN panel is actually better.<br /><br />Anyway, to each their own.  I do miss the glory days of the old ThinkPad T-series as much as anyone, but I don't think the changes that some would describe as a gradual decline in quality are a result of clueless management not realizing what their customers want.  To the contrary, they know very well what most of their customers want and it looks less and less like what we want, because people willing to pay a premium for indestructible laptops with perfect keyboards are an anachronism.  Thin and light increasingly trumps everything else, even among ThinkPad buyers.
