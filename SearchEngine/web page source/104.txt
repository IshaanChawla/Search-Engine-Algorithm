		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">The new wave: 20 indie games to watch</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Ahead of the E3 blockbusters, we look at some intriguing smaller up-and-comers. </h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by         <span class="author" itemprop="name">Kyle Orland and Sam Machkovech</span>

  </p>

			<div class="mobile-meta-controls">

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    



    <p>With E3 coming up next month, we'll soon be gearing up to bring you word on the glitziest, biggest-budget blockbusters that the major publishers can currently cook up. Before that happens, though, we wanted to put the spotlight on some of the smaller, possibly overlooked titles we've been enjoying at festivals and elsewhere within the past year. The 20 independent games that follow show what the smaller, more inventive side of the industry has in the works. If you ask us, it's every bit as promising as what we expect to encounter during the summer's biggest blockbuster games event.</p>
<h2>Aboard the Lookinglass</h2>
<p><b>Developer:</b> thehen<br />
<b>Platforms:</b> Rift/LeapMotion<br />
<b>Available:</b> TBA<br />
<a href="http://thehen.itch.io/lookinglass">Website</a><br />
<figure class="video" style="width:100%"><iframe style="display:block" type="text/html" width="100%" height="360" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/LX9i7ULY8uw?start=0&wmode=transparent" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe></figure><br />
Developing natural control schemes is still the big question mark for a lot of virtual reality headsets. Leap Motion is trying to solve that problem, hosting a recent game jam to show off its Kinect-like tracker as a way to let players put their hands into VR simply by holding them in front of their face.</p>
<p><i>Aboard the Lookinglass</i> uses that clever control solution for some inventive time-bending gameplay. Looking through the translucent, red-tinted image of your left hand creates a window into the past, while looking through your blue right hand lets you see into the future. This lets you work out some intricate puzzles by viewing an intact version of a broken picture in the past, for instance, or reaching into the future where a barrier is no longer in your way.</p>
<p>It's all in service of a story based around solving the mystery of the abandoned spaceship setting, complete with a <i>Portal</i>-style disembodied computer voice guiding you. While there are probably few people with the Rift and Leap Motion setup needed to play it, those with the means will get a puzzle adventure unlike anything else out there.</p>
<h2>Anarcute</h2>
<p><b>Developer:</b> Anarteam<br />
<b>Platforms:</b> Xbox One, Windows<br />
<b>Available:</b> Summer 2015<br />
<a href="http://anarcute.com/">Website</a></p>
<figure class="video" style="width:100%"><iframe style="display:block" type="text/html" width="100%" height="360" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/7tn72BljCH0?start=0&wmode=transparent" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe></figure>
<p>You can count the great games for the Wii U on one hand, but you’d probably relegate Platinum Games’ <em>Wonderful 101</em> to a pinky. It wasn’t bad by any stretch, but this control-a-crowd game’s wonky use of the Wii U Gamepad and otherwise thin action made it hard to fall in love with as a major retail game. We might’ve liked it more as a slimmed-down downloadable game—and we’re getting pretty much that with <em>Anarcute</em>.</p>
<p>Players must lead and enlist a growing mob of protesters—wearing cute animal masks in colorful cities, at that—by running through streets and alleys to find new converts. Once you have a pack of rioters, you then use the city’s resources, from benches to traffic cones to entire buildings, to take down oppressive riot cops. As the game progresses, the cops get stronger (watch out for choppers and laser tripwires), but so do the 99 percent’s battle options and powers.</p>
<p>This is an immediately inviting game, thanks to its “cute rioter” aesthetic, simple combat controls, and bubbly animations. Its full-screen chaos evokes the best memories of <em>Rampage</em>’s arcade city-destruction system.</p>
<h2>Bit Ball</h2>
<p><b>Developer:</b> CJ Kimberlain<br />
<b>Platforms:</b> Windows, Mac<br />
<b>Available:</b> TBA<br />
<a href="http://bitballgame.com/">Website</a></p>
<div style="width: 580px; " class="wp-video"><!--[if lt IE 9]><script>document.createElement('video');</script><![endif]-->
<video class="wp-video-shortcode" id="video-666081-1" width="580" height="328" preload="metadata" controls="controls"><source type="video/mp4" src="//cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/CEtGRTIVEAAMuw0.mp4?_=1" /><a href="//cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/CEtGRTIVEAAMuw0.mp4">//cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/CEtGRTIVEAAMuw0.mp4</a></video></div>
<p>The 8-bit sports renaissance is in full swing, and we couldn’t be happier. We’ve already written about a few modern takes on the days of NES <em>Ice Hockey</em>, including <em>Videoball</em> and <em>Hokra</em>, and we’ve added a few more gems to our growing pile of couch-friendly indie sports.</p>
<p>We've already shined a spotlight on <em><a href="http://arstechnica.com/gaming/2014/12/sony-bets-it-all-on-indie-at-playstations-vegas-expo/">Rocket League</a></em>, which is now in a semi-open beta on PS4, and while that game's great, we like two other games that revolve around putting a ball into a goal: <em><a href="http://tippinggoat.com/blog/super-slam-dunk-touchdown/">Super Slam Dunk Touchdown</a></em> and <em>Bit Ball. </em>Our fondness for the former comes mostly from its resemblance to <em>Arch Rivals</em>, the arcade predecessor to <em>NBA Jam</em>. Players dress up as one of many sports archetypes, including basketball, baseball, hockey, and soccer, to tackle each other and attempt to either kick a ball into a giant goal or shoot it into a hoop. We love its sense of humor, but we also appreciated the arcade action and teamplay challenges required to win a match.</p>
<p>In terms of pure gameplay, however, <em>Bit Ball</em> wins out by adding just enough juice to the formula that Xbox One indie gem <em>#idarb</em> tried to get right last year. Like that game, players hop around <em>Smash Bros.</em>-style inside of a small 2D arena to grab a ball and aim it at a goal, but <em>Bit Ball</em> refines the “shooting” process by having players transform into a turret whenever they touch the ball—or when they land on top of a foe, at which point the winning tackler shoots their opponent in whatever direction they want. This simple, mechanical tweak, plus a very small, choice number of power-ups for speed and power, made us bug-eyed in delight after our first match.</p>
<h2>Close Your</h2>
<p><b>Developer:</b> Goodbyeworld Games<br />
<b>Platforms:</b> Windows<br />
<b>Available:</b> TBA<br />
<a href="http://www.closeyour.com/">Website</a></p>
<figure class="video" style="width:100%"><iframe style="display:block" type="text/html" width="100%" height="360" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/eNQBKBXOul4?start=0&wmode=transparent" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe></figure>
<p>The story-first, text-driven gaming genre has exploded in recent years, and while we can always make room for a good bit of <em>Zork</em>-ian adventure or Twine-powered fiction, we always prefer a creative twist on clicking through text. Quite frankly, we don’t know that we’ll ever see anything as creative as <em>Close Your</em> ever again, at least in this genre.</p>
<p>Players proceed through an average person’s life, making decisions as they age that fork the story in wildly different directions, but the decision-making process is stressful for a major reason. The game requires a webcam, because it watches your real-life eyes, and every time you blink, the game fast-forwards an indeterminate amount of time. Maybe a few minutes, maybe a few years—the flash depends on your age in the story, and the developers have made some fast-forwards extreme on purpose.</p>
<p>If you’re looking for a game that ruminates artfully on the ways work, family, and love wither in the sands of time, look no further.</p>
<h2>Donut County</h2>
<p><b>Developer:</b> Ben Esposito<br />
<b>Platforms:</b> Windows, Mac<br />
<b>Available:</b> 2015<br />
<a href="http://donutcounty.com/">Website</a></p>
<figure class="video" style="width:100%"><iframe style="display:block" type="text/html" width="100%" height="360" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/ZNlR96LspY0?start=0&wmode=transparent" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe></figure>
<p>Boing Boing captured this game perfectly with <a href="http://boingboing.net/2015/03/18/whats-it-like-to-be-a-hole.html">the headline</a> "What's it like to be a hole." Yes, in <i>Donut County</i> you control a hole, moving underneath bright, candy colored environments and swallowing up anything that will fit inside you. The hole's size increases every time it swallows something, allowing you to slowly build up from swallowing tiny things like carrots and spare tires to big things like cows and entire houses.</p>
<p>There's more than a bit of a <i>Katamari Damacy</i> vibe to the proceedings, and there's a similar sense of satisfaction to be found in simply gobbling up everything in sight. But there's also a puzzle-y twist to each level. In one area, for instance, you need to figure out how to fill the hole with water, so you can hydrate another portion of the map and set off the events that you need to continue. It's a brief moment of thought and reflection... then you can get right back to swallowing up everything in sight!</p>
<h2>Downwell</h2>
<p><b>Developer:</b> Moppin<br />
<b>Platforms:</b> Windows, iOS, Android<br />
<b>Available:</b> Q2 2015<br />
<a href="http://downwellgame.com/">Website</a><br />
<figure class="video" style="width:100%"><iframe style="display:block" type="text/html" width="100%" height="360" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/nexG5HGB__g?start=0&wmode=transparent" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe></figure></p>
<p><i>Downwell</i>'s website describes it as "a game about a young man going down an endless well with gun-boots." It's hard to beat that direct description.</p>
<p>The game stands out partly for its stark, three-color visual style, all black and white with occasional flashes of red for accents. The game's aspect ratio is also taller than it is wide (PC players will probably want to turn their monitor to the side). The minimalist style fits with the minimalist controls, which feature only a single button that serves as both a downward attack and a jump/hover function. Navigating the randomly generated, endless caves with those limited abilities is an equal mix of twitch reflexes and quickly planning elegant paths through the maze-like structures and enemies that quickly surround you.</p>
<p>There's a bit of character advancement in picking up money and items from shops. In the best tradition of games like <i>Spelunky</i>, though, the real advancement is in the player, who gets a little bit better with each avoidable death until situations that once seemed overwhelming become routine and manageable. I much preferred playing on a gamepad to trying to manage on a smartphone, but either way <i>Downwell</i> has that addictive, one-more-game action down pat.</p>
