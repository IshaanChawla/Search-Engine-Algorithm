		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">EU carriers plan to block ads, demand money from Google</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Blocking scheme arises with net neutrality rules not yet finalized in Europe.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    



    <p>Multiple mobile operators in Europe plan to block advertising on their networks, with one of them planning to target Google's ad network to force the company to give up a cut of its ad revenue, according to a <a href="http://www.ft.com/cms/s/0/7010ae7a-f4c6-11e4-8a42-00144feab7de.html">report yesterday in the <em>Financial Times</em></a>."An executive at a European carrier confirmed that it and several of its peers are planning to start blocking adverts this year," the newspaper reported. "The executive said that the carrier will initially launch an advertising-free service for customers on an opt-in basis. But it is also considering a more radical idea that it calls 'the bomb', which would apply across its entire network of millions of subscribers at once. The idea is to specifically target Google, blocking advertising on its websites in an attempt to force the company into giving up a cut of its revenues."</p>
<p>Blocking ads "just for an hour or a day" might be enough to bring Google to the negotiating table, the executive told the newspaper.</p>
<p>While such a scheme might violate <a href="http://arstechnica.com/business/2015/02/fcc-votes-for-net-neutrality-a-ban-on-paid-fast-lanes-and-title-ii/">net neutrality rules in the United States</a>, Europe doesn't have anything comprehensive on the books despite years of discussion.</p>
<p>"There are no clear rules on net neutrality today at EU level, leaving 96 percent of Europeans without legal protection for their right to access the full open Internet," <a href="https://ec.europa.eu/digital-agenda/en/eu-actions">the European Commission said</a>.</p>
<p>Google sent a response to Ars, which said, “People pay for mobile internet packages so they can access the apps, video streaming, webmail and other services they love, many of which are funded by ads. Google and other Web companies invest heavily in developing these services—and in the behind-the-scenes infrastructure to deliver them.”</p>
<p>The blocking technology was developed by a company called <a href="http://www.getshine.com/">Shine</a>. "The software prevents most types of advertising from loading in web pages and apps, though it does not interfere with 'in-feed' ads of the kind used by Facebook and Twitter," the <em>Financial Times</em> wrote.</p>
<p>Shine confirmed it is working with a number of mobile operators but declined to identify them, according to the report.</p>
<p>In 2013, the French ISP Free turned on ad-blocking by default but <a href="http://arstechnica.com/business/2013/01/frances-second-largest-isp-suspends-ad-blocking-for-now/">suspended the practice after controversy</a>.</p>

Since i have a FreeNAS server on all the time i also set up a jail running two proxies: <a href="http://www.squid-cache.org/" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">Squid</a> for caching, and <a href="http://www.privoxy.org/" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">Privoxy</a> (as a parent proxy for Squid) to match and strip ads from hosts that aren't on the DNS-blocked list.<br /><br />This combination works extremely well and i rarely encounter an ad, but i'm really fighting a battle i'm doomed to lose.<br /><br />Privoxy works by being able to examine the HTML of pages, so is only effective for non-secure pages.  With the push towards encryption everywhere Privoxy will become less and less effective.<br /><br />If i were willing to create a wildcard certificate encompassing every domain, and have all the client devices in my network trust it (and if Privoxy supported such a thing), i could have Privoxy man-in-the-middle all the TLS connections which would allow me to continue stripping ads, but i'm not willing to break TLS to do this.<br /><br />With Privoxy out of the running when it comes to stripping ads on secure sites i'm left with DNS blocking.  Which is all fine and dandy when it comes to blocking hosts that do nothing but serve ads, but what happens when a big player that also provides valuable services starts to serve ads from the same machine?<br /><br />I might be willing to block pagead.l.google.com on the DNS-level but i'm certainly not going to block mail.google.com.<br /><br />I really don't see how Shine could fare any better with this than me but if they can i'd love to learn how and stave of the adpocalypse for a while longer.
