		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">Are galaxies strangled or stripped of gas? A “murder mystery” solved</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">New result explains why most galaxies stop forming new stars.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by     <a itemprop="url" href="http://arstechnica.com/author/xaq/" rel="author"><span itemprop="name">Xaq Rzetelny</span></a>
      -  <span class="date" data-time="1431967788">May 18, 2015 4:49 pm UTC</span>
  </p>

			<div class="mobile-meta-controls">

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    
<figure class="intro-image image center full-width" style="width:640px">
      <img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/PIA10113_ip-640x510.jpg" width="640" height="510">
  
    <figcaption class="caption">
			<div class="caption-text">Here, blue represents UV light, typical of locations with newly formed stars. This galaxy is clearly nowhere close to strangulation.</div>
	
			<div class="caption-credit">
							<a rel="nofollow" href="http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/spaceimages/details.php?id=PIA10113">NASA</a>
				</div>
	  </figcaption>
  </figure>




    <p>A group of researchers seems to have solved an old murder mystery: do galaxies die suddenly when their gas supply is expelled at once? Or do they die little by little after their gas supply from outside is cut off? “Die” here refers to the galaxy’s ability to form new stars. They'll continue to exist for a long time after this death, but they'll move into the population of “quiescent” galaxies with old stars that can expect few siblings to join them.</p>
<p>Stars are formed by the contraction of gas, so if a galaxy has little gas to contract, it can’t make any new stars. If some process forced all of a galaxy’s gas out suddenly, then star production would come to a sudden halt. If the galaxy's outside gas supply cut off, it would still have gas left within the galaxy to work with, but star formation would slowly grind to a halt because of this strangulation.</p>
<p>So the difference between the methods of dying is a bit like having the air violently sucked out of your lungs in outer space (gas ejection) or holding your breath underwater (strangulation).</p>
<h2>Going metal</h2>
<p>To find out which of the two scenarios is actually happening, researchers examined local galaxies from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS). They examined a sample of 22,618 quiescent galaxies and compared them to 3,905 galaxies that are still undergoing star formation. They examined the spectra of the light from these galaxies to determine metalicity. In astrophysics, any element above helium on the periodic table is considered a metal. Most of the gas in the Universe is either hydrogen or helium, but some of it is converted into metals via fusion in the core of a star.</p>
<p>When stars later die, they can release material back out into interstellar space—gas that has more metals than the star had when it first formed. New stars may then form from this metallic gas, and the results will have higher metalicity, meaning more heavy elements.</p>
<p>Under the two scenarios of galaxy death—strangulation and ejection of the gas—researchers would expect to see different metalicities. If the gas is ejected, then the metalicity stays the same as it was just before the gas was ejected, since few new stars can be formed to increase the metalicity. In the strangulation scenario, however, the metalicity continues to increase after the flow of gas stops because new stars are still being produced. In fact, the metalicity actually increases faster after the gas flow has stopped, since there's no gas flowing in to dilute it out.</p>
<p>It’s a bit like pouring both water and orange juice into a jug, where the orange juice represents metals. If you suddenly stop pouring the water but keep pouring the orange juice at the same rate, the jug’s contents will quickly become more orangey than watery.</p>
<h2>Whodunit?</h2>
<p>The results of the researchers' analysis are decisive. The metalicity of the galaxies’ spectra “clearly reveals that strangulation is the primary mechanism responsible for quenching star formation,” the authors write in their paper. This strangulation process takes about four billion years to stop star formation altogether. (Coincidentally, it takes about four minutes for a human to die while being strangled).</p>
<p>Importantly, these results only apply to galaxies below about 100 billion times the mass of the Sun (solar masses). Above that mass, galaxies become scarce, meaning that strangulation is the most common mechanism for halting star formation in the Universe, since it applies to the most common types of galaxy.</p>
<p>Larger galaxies, such as giant elliptical galaxies, shut down much quicker than the four billion years of strangulation (a fact that was established prior to this study). They likely formed by different mechanisms as well, so it’s not necessarily surprising that they would also die by different mechanisms.</p>
<p>The next question is what causes the gas to be cut off. It probably has something to do with the immediate environment around the galaxy. Galaxies in groups or clusters may be cut off from the surrounding gas either by ram pressure (a pressure felt by any body moving through a fluid) or by tidal forces exerted by surrounding galaxies. In some cases, ejection of a galaxy’s gas may also contribute. Future work should conclusively answer this question, and further detail the role of the strangulation in ending star formation in galaxies.</p>

As these analogies draw further speculation and invite greater research, they have their purpose, but they should in no way lead us to believe that we can really know what's happening to distant galaxies based solely on the tiny snapshot of what's happening quintillions of miles away that we obtain with our telescopes. My pet perspective and theory is that galaxies never die but continue to expand and grow forever. We only think they die because everything around us dies and breaks down. Our current intellectual capacity doesn't account for the fact that something may exist forever. My theory is just a theory and should be treated as such, but so is this &quot;group of researchers&quot; theory. They've done their diligence in trying to explain the unexplainable, which is an admirable venture, but their findings should be taken with great skepticism given the innumerable limitations of their capacity to observe.
