		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">Coast to coast, climate to climate, present to past</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">This road trip took me places I already knew, both geographically and emotionally. </h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by     <a itemprop="url" href="http://arstechnica.com/author/sean-gallagher/" rel="author"><span itemprop="name">Sean Gallagher</span></a>

  </p>

			<div class="mobile-meta-controls">

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    
<figure class="intro-image image center full-width" style="width:640px">
      <img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/IMG_0673-640x427.jpg" width="640" height="427">
  
    <figcaption class="caption">
			<div class="caption-text">A view of the west span of the Bay Bridge, facing San Francisco, taken while technically not tresspassing on the Coast Guard station on Yerba Buena Island. </div>
	
			<div class="caption-credit">
							Sean Gallagher				</div>
	  </figcaption>
  </figure>



      <div class="article-intro">With Ars sending writers around the globe to visit GE research centers, we wanted our readers to share in with some of the experiences we're having during these travels. These blog posts are meant to convey some highlights rather than being an exhaustive account of our trip.</div>
    
    <p>Visiting Contra Costa County in California is not unpleasant, especially when you work for Ars. There's a critical mass of Ars staffers in the Bay area, so I was not entirely without social contact outside my assigned tasks in San Ramon. Let's just say that David Kravets throws a hell of a pizza party and leave it at that.</p>
<figure class="image right full full-width" style="width:630px"><img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/pimpcon.jpg" width="630" height="311"><figcaption class="caption"><div class="caption-text">A few Arsians at Casa de Kravets—from left to right, Cyrus Farivar, John (Jay) Timmer, Tiffany Kelly, Sean Gallagher, Dan Goodin, Joe Mullin, and David Kravets Esquire.</div> </figcaption></figure>
<p>There's also the scenery. After the first day of shooting, we took a trip to get some "b-roll" footage of the Bay Bridge and soon found ourselves on Yerba Buena Island looking for someplace to park. There may have been some questionable navigation involved, but I believe we were never technically trespassing on federal land. The California Highway Patrol car that stopped nearby was just there because the trooper was taking a smoke break and snapping pictures of San Francisco with his smartphone.</p>
<p>In many ways, the Bay area is a sort of home away from home for me—I worked for a company in Palo Alto for a few years and spent a quarter of my time there. I have family in San Francisco and Santa Cruz. My wife and I honeymooned in San Francisco and used to spend a good chunk of our summers there (thanks largely to JavaOne).</p>
<figure class="image right large full-width" style="width:640px"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-08.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="3456" data-width="5184"><img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-08-640x427.jpg" width="640" height="427" srcset="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-08-1280x853.jpg 2x"></a><figcaption class="caption"><div class="caption-text"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-08.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="3456" data-width="5184">Enlarge</a> <span class="sep">/</span> The hills outside San Ramon are a bit more welcoming than the snow I left behind in Baltimore.</div> </figcaption></figure>
<p>But as comfortable as I am in northern California, it's still a bit alien to me. Sitting in a parking lot in San Ramon at 6:30am, watching a landscaping team armed with leaf blowers and headlamps clearing dead vegetation from pavement before dawn was... surreal.</p>
<p>On the other hand, going to Niskayuna, New York for the next leg of my trip triggered all sorts of other personal memories. It wasn't so much Niskayuna itself, but everything that's around it—plus the weather.</p>
<figure class="image right large full-width" style="width:640px"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff5.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="1080" data-width="1918"><img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff5-640x360.jpg" width="640" height="360" srcset="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff5-1280x721.jpg 2x"></a><figcaption class="caption"><div class="caption-text"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff5.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="1080" data-width="1918">Enlarge</a> <span class="sep">/</span> The view from Niskayuna.</div> </figcaption></figure>
<p>While I mostly grew up on Long Island, I spent the end of my high school years and much of my summers and winters while in college in what some refer to as "the 518." This huge area code zone covers everything from Albany north to the Canadian border—including the Adirondacks, the North Country, and my parents' home in Plattsburgh. Schenectady and Niskayuna are wedged alongside Albany at the southern edge of the 518. I have roots there; my father's family is from a few hours west, and my grandfather used to have Schenectady on his linen route back before paper towels and World War II. For most of my life, I feel like I've driven through or around Schenectady and Albany thousands of times.</p>
<p>When I went to college, I used to joke, I decided to go to Wisconsin because it was warmer—at least warm enough that I could put my cross-country skis to regular use getting around campus between November and April. <span style="line-height: 19.0909080505371px;">When deep winter comes to this part of upstate New York—real upstate, north of the Catskills and far enough north that you'd might as well drive to Montreal as to New York City—the daylight is precious, blue skies are a tease, and if you don't like winter you're really screwed. </span>Fortunately, I do like winter, and like other northeastern transplants to the Mid-Atlantic, I generally deride my chosen hometown's snow removal and the apparent idiocy of Baltimore and DC drivers when the first flakes fall from the sky. I'd never heard of schools opening late for slush until I moved to Baltimore (though the one time it snowed three inches in Norfolk was utterly hysterical).</p>
<figure class="image right large full-width" style="width:640px"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-11.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="2448" data-width="3264"><img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-11-640x480.jpg" width="640" height="480" srcset="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-11-1280x960.jpg 2x"></a><figcaption class="caption"><div class="caption-text"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/gallagher-ge-11.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="2448" data-width="3264">Enlarge</a> <span class="sep">/</span> Welcome to Schenectady.</div> </figcaption></figure>
<p><span style="line-height: 1.5;">But all the romance of winter is dead for me by February, and it's down to whether or not I am planning to off Punxsutawney Phil if he sees his shadow. And the last time I had been in the 518 at this time of year was three years ago for my brother's funeral. Let's just say that I have a personal grudge against the long stretch of dark that is late winter in New York from the Mohawk to the St. Lawrence.</span></p>
<p>The schedule I had for my one full day and two nights was not exactly conducive to getting a lot of sightseeing done, but late February isn't exactly a good time for sightseeing in northern New York to begin with. There had been talk of driving up to look at the Clinton County wind farm for some footage of the wind turbines there, but given that it was 40 below on the Fahrenheit scale when we arrived and the wind farm was 150 miles north in the 518, we were easily convinced that it might not be all that productive.</p>
<p>The sky was pitch dark by the time we were done shooting in Schenectady, and the video crew was too tired for a promised trip to the bowling lanes. I considered driving into Albany for dinner, or even into Troy. There's a relatively recent addition there to the Dinosaur Bar-B-Que chain, which started in Syracuse and is now spreading down the East coast (there's even one opening in Baltimore).</p>
<p>Instead, I checked in with my parents, who were escaping the final blast of winter down in Florida. And they told me to go to Reel Seafood in Colonie, just over a mile from the airport.</p>
<p>It's in a stretch of road I know well—right off the Northway (I-87), in the line of hotels and retail on the fringes of Colonie Center, the suburban Albany retail mecca where I spent many an hour sitting in the parking lot waiting for my wife to check out of the Christmas Tree Shop. This is not exactly where I would expect to find a decent seafood restaurant, given that the other nearby options were national chains or hotel restaurants. And it's over 150 miles to the nearest thing you could call a "sea" from Colonie.</p>
<figure class="image right large full-width" style="width:640px"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff6.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="3264" data-width="2448"><img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff6-640x853.jpg" width="640" height="853" srcset="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff6-1280x1707.jpg 2x"></a><figcaption class="caption"><div class="caption-text"><a href="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/blogstuff6.jpg" class="enlarge" data-height="3264" data-width="2448">Enlarge</a> <span class="sep">/</span> Oysters in Albany. Win.</div> </figcaption></figure>
<p>So, of course I went. And it was pretty damn good. I was at the bar, eating alone, but I had a cocktail and a half-dozen oysters, and then a whole roasted bronzini, and I was suddenly not feeling the dark anymore. I went back to my hotel room, packed, and tried to catch a few hours sleep before my 4am wakeup call.</p>
<p>A pair of Bombardier Dash-8 flights later, I was back in Baltimore, mission complete. The virtual baton will soon be back to Dr. Timmer, who will have the great honor of carrying the Ars banner to Bangalore, India for our next road trip. That's a bit more terra incognita for Ars than upstate New York.</p>
