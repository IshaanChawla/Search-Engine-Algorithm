		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">Pandora, fresh off one copyright win, loses its rate case to BMI</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Two songwriters' groups get two very different results.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by     <a itemprop="url" href="http://arstechnica.com/author/joe-mullin/" rel="author"><span itemprop="name">Joe Mullin</span></a>

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    



    <p>Songwriters' group Broadcast Music Inc. (BMI) has beaten online radio provider Pandora after a two-year legal battle, winning a substantially larger copyright royalty rate of 2.5 percent.</p>
<p>That's a large increase from the 1.75 percent Pandora was paying before. It's also a stark contrast to Pandora's win in a similar case against BMI's rival, the American Society of Composers Authors and Publishers, or ASCAP. It was just last week that a federal appeals court <a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2015/05/appeals-court-upholds-pandoras-victory-over-songwriters/">upheld Pandora's win</a> in that case, finding that the royalty rate should rise to only 1.85 percent.</p>
<p>The judge's opinion in <em>BMI v. Pandora</em> isn't yet public, but both sides have put out statements about the results.</p>
<p>"Today’s decision is an enormous victory for the more than 650,000 songwriters, composers and publishers that BMI has the privilege to represent," BMI said in a statement. "The Court resoundingly agreed with BMI, supporting our position that 2.5% was 'reasonable, and indeed at the low end of the range of fees of recent licenses.' This is an important step forward in valuing music in the digital age."</p>
<p>Pandora emphasized that the fight isn't over, and it intends to take it to the US Court of Appeals for the 2nd Circuit. "We remain confident in our legal position," said a Pandora spokesperson. "We disagree with the Court’s ruling and will appeal to the same court that ruled in Pandora's favor in the ASCAP case last week. We strongly believe the benchmarks cited by the court do not provide an appropriate competitive foundation for a market rate."</p>
<p>Lawyers for Pandora had argued that the judge in the BMI case should follow the 2nd Circuit ruling in the BMI case, but BMI lawyers successfully argued that there was no need. The 2nd Circuit only reviewed the ASCAP results for "clear error," and the judge decided the result in the <em>BMI v. Pandora</em> case only after its own weeks-long trial.</p>
<p>The differing results in two similar cases mean that Pandora's copyright battles are far from over. The songwriter cases don't affect the royalties Pandora has to pay to record labels, which constitute its largest expense.</p>
<p>The Justice Department is <a href="http://www.justice.gov/atr/cases/ascapbmi/">reviewing the consent decrees</a> that relate to BMI and ASCAP, a process that began last June, but it hasn't given any indication as to when the review will be complete.</p>

Same goes for any other streaming radio (Shoutcast-style, etc).  <br /><br />I can understand higher rates for on-demand services like Spotify where you can pick exactly what you want to hear and that could conceivably translate to lost sales of mp3's, CD, etc.  But for radio, the argument has always been that you license the content for broadcast but don't need to pay a high royalty rate since you add the value of promotion.  It's always pissed me off that I had to shut down my old streaming station years ago when it went from an affordable license fee to ASCAP/BMI to a per-listener-per-song royalty which is way outside the budget of most hobbyist/enthusiast streamers.  So many online stations solved the problem of nothing good on the radio and were rewarded with cost-prohibitive licensing rates that traditional radio would have balked at.
