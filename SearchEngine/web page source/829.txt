<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>The federal standards (that states lose highway funds for not following) are that you cannot purchase or publicly possess alcoholic beverages under the age of 21. Technically this is implemented as state laws, but it applies in all 50 states and DC. That means neither of you can buy alcohol legally. In addition, the general rule is that you can't ask someone over 21 to buy it for you. There is no permit process or anything like that to get around this. Sorry.</p>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>Age limits and such like are always those of the place you are in. While you are in another country you have to obey the local laws on drink, and other stuff, whatever your laws are back home. The good news is that you can do things that are legal in the place you are, even if they are illegal back home (with some exceptions), which is great news if you are in the US and have a burning desire to fire a machine gun. (Yes, there are places in the US where young people are allowed to openly carry a loaded machine gun in public but not buy a beer)</p>

<p>There are some states which technically allow you to drink, either with parents permission and/or in private, but unless you are travelling with an over-21 you won't be able to get round the prohibition on purchase. And even if you have an over-21 with you, beware that in some places purchasing alcohol for a minor is a serious offence.</p>

But <strong>you will not be allowed to do this at a bar!! <td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>Simply put, no.  </p>

<p>As an UK immigrant myself, first arriving at age 19 (and effectively drinking in the UK since age 16) I certainly experienced this first hand.  You have to be 21.</p>

<p>Significantly, age is nearly always determined by requesting and reviewing a drivers license which has a picture of the person plus the date of birth.</p>

<p>This is unlike my upbringing in the UK where drivers licenses didn't have a picture and a lot of times the 'old enough to drink?' was based on looks alone.</p>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>The short answer to the question is no: local laws apply everywhere you go, and the standard minimum drinking age in the United States is 21. </p>

<p>The long answer is that yes, <strong>it is absolutely legal for someone under the age of 21 to purchase and consume alcohol in the United States— in certain areas, under the right circumstances</strong>. But those circumstances will not apply to most tourists, e.g. working as part of a law enforcement operation, being U.S. military personnel on a base, or being <a href="https://www.hotelschool.cornell.edu/academics/courses/course.html?id=HADM4300" rel="nofollow">enrolled in a curriculum</a> where consumption of alcohol is required.</p>

<p>Drinking age is a state-level matter (which <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._history_of_alcohol_minimum_purchase_age_by_state" rel="nofollow">varies over time</a>), while the particularities of when and where you can purchase, and what and how you consume are hyperlocal. The National Minimum Drinking Age Act of 1984 (<a href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/23/158" rel="nofollow">23 U.S.C. §158</a>) requires states to set their drinking age at 21 at the risk of losing their federal highway funding. This was affirmed as constitutional in <em>South Dakota v. Dole</em> (<a href="http://caselaw.lp.findlaw.com/scripts/getcase.pl?court=US&amp;vol=483&amp;invol=203" rel="nofollow">483 U.S. 203 (1987)</a>) and was later explicated with regulation. But while the <em>purchase</em> of alcohol by people under 21 is banned essentially everywhere, and <em>possession</em> in most places, there is some variation on <em>consumption</em> and on <em>internal possession</em> (i.e. evidence of recent consumption, like your wine tongue or beer breath).</p>

<hr>

<p>I am not a lawyer, and what follows is not legal advice.</p>

<p>People under 21 <em>may</em> be allowed to consume some kinds of alcohol in some parts of the country</p>

<ul>
<li>for an established religious purpose, e.g. <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/1990/04/11/garden/sacramental-wine-lowest-profile-of-all.html" rel="nofollow">sacramental wine</a></li>
<li>when accompanied by a parent/guardian or spouse who is 21 or older</li>
<li>for medical purposes when administered by a licensed healthcare professional or institution (this was also true during Prohibition)</li>
<li>on the premises of a military installation, if the state minimum age has been waived by the commanding officer of that installation</li>
<li>on tribal lands (e.g. Indian reservations) which set a lower drinking age</li>
</ul>

<p>States also have strict laws concerning the furnishing of alcohol to underage people, misrepresentation of age, and the use of false or altered identification. The standard for blood alcohol content is often lower for underage drivers (e.g. .08% for adults but .02% for minors). In some states it is illegal even to <em>attempt</em> to purchase or consume if under 21; in Texas, for example, it is a Class C misdemeanor.</p>

<p>The <strong><a href="https://alcoholpolicy.niaaa.nih.gov/state_profiles_of_underage_drinking_laws.html" rel="nofollow">State Profiles of Underage Drinking Laws</a></strong> project of the National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (of the National Institutes of Health) provides you with state-by-state overviews of the specific laws, definitions, and penalties that may apply in the states or territories you will visit. For example, some states do not penalize consumption in private gatherings in private residences, so if the police were called, <em>you</em> would not be trouble for drinking, though the person who provided you with the drink would be. But as a foreign visitor, you run the risk of being expelled from the country, and so I would exercise an abundance of caution.</p>

<td class="answercell">
    <div class="post-text" itemprop="text">
<p>No, you will not be able to drink legally within the United States.  </p>

<p>Purchasing alcoholic beverages as a minor, or knowingly purchasing alcohol <em>for</em> a minor, are both criminal offenses in all 50 states.  </p>

<p>Conversely, if someone underage were to travel to Australia and drink with <em>you</em>, then as long as they're old enough under Australian law, they are allowed to do so.  </p>

<p>Drinking laws apply only within the jurisdiction in which they're enforced, and largely apply to the <em>sale</em> of alcohol, not to the consumption of alcohol.  </p>

<p>You may, depending on the exact legislation of the state you are visiting, be able to bypass this regulation by visiting someone in the states that <em>already owns</em> alcohol.  Depending on the state legislation, as long as they are 21 or over, they may be allowed to give you alcohol under the table.  </p>

<p>If you don't know anyone in the States, you may still be able to bypass this law by visiting an <a href="http://www.en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_American_gaming" rel="nofollow">Indian Casino</a>.  Because they are not owned by a State, they have their own regulations on drinking, an may be willing to sell you alcohol.  </p>

<p>Note that alcohol sale is still strictly enforced within the states, and if anyone finds out that you are planning to bypass the law in this way, you may face travel restrictions.  Drink at your own risk, and <strong>do not plan to purchase or carry any alcohol while travelling</strong>.  </p>

<td class="answercell">

<p>Typically you are always subject to the laws of the jurisdiction you are in unless you have <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_immunity" rel="nofollow">diplomatic immunity</a>.</p>

<p>So if you are in the US, the US drinking laws apply to you.  If you are in Germany, the German drinking laws apply to you.</p>

<p>Your nationality doesn't matter - the laws of the place you are in matter.</p>
