<p>When Marty McFly traveled to 2015 in <em>Back to the Future Part II</em>, one of the most enviable aspects of a 1980s-envisioned future was the high-tech clothing. He slipped on a jacket that auto adjusted to his body and dried in seconds after being emerged in water. His shoelaces automatically tied.</p>
<p>It all seemed very novel and exciting. Who wants boring clothes made out of cotton when you can have clothing that talks to you and has a function besides covering your skin? Well, <em>Back to the Future</em>’s prediction of our current day fashion wasn’t too far off from reality—at least that's reality in San Francisco.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.sv-fashionweek.com/">Silicon Valley Fashion Week?</a>, a three-day event held this week in the city’s Mission District, showcased some of the latest futuristic apparel. There was a fiber optic dress. There were 3D-printed jewelry and hats. There were <a href="https://zackees.com">turn signal gloves</a>. There was a hydration pack “reimagined for desert dancing” (really).</p>
<p>Betabrand, the San Francisco-based company behind such innovations as <a href="https://www.betabrand.com/womens/pants/womens-plaid-boot-cut-dress-pant-yoga-pants.html">yoga work pants</a> and <a href="https://www.betabrand.com/crowdfunding/mens-business-suit-onesie-hybrid.html">a onesie suit</a>, announced that they were holding the event three weeks ago. The lead image for the event showed a drone flying down the catwalk above a woman with robotic legs. Naturally, Ars RSVP’ed with hopes that we’d be seated next to an AI (alas, just fellow reporters for tech pubs.)</p>
<p>Each night had a different theme, from commuter gear to wearable tech to crowdfunded clothing. Most of the models were humans; a few of them were drones. In fact, the organizers claimed that it was the first fashion show to use drone models. The whole event had a tongue-in-cheek feel, but the designers seemed humbled to show off their products in front of 1,200 people each night. A few designers even stopped by the press area to talk to reporters individually. It was clear that as the market for 3D-printed clothing and wearable tech grows, we may see more events like SVFW.</p>
<p>Chris Lindland, founder of Betabrand, called the event a “grand experiment.”</p>
<p>“This was just an idea I had,” he told Ars. “It was very much, ‘I think I can make this work.’ In the short amount of time, it came together beautifully. “</p>
<p>It's hard to say if the runway fashion featured in the show will trickle down into the streets. Some items, like a <a href="http://tespack.com/us/">messenger bag that charges electronic devices</a> with solar power, seemed practical. A 3D-printed hat with holes at the top or a glowing vest straight out of <em>Tron</em>? Not really. Still, some of the more impractical designs were the most inspiring, particularly clothing that can track heart rates and <a href="http://sensoree.com/moodsweater">moods</a>. Not exactly like an auto adjusting jacket, but still pretty cool.</p>
