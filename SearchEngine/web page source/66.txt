		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">Engineered virus protects bacteria while eliminating antibiotic resistance</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">CRISPR/Cas9 system isn't just for engineering human genomes.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by     <a itemprop="url" href="http://arstechnica.com/author/john-timmer/" rel="author"><span itemprop="name">John Timmer</span></a>
      -  <span class="date" data-time="1432041376">May 19, 2015 1:16 pm UTC</span>
  </p>

			<div class="mobile-meta-controls">

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    
<figure class="intro-image image center full-width" style="width:640px">
      <img src="http://cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/antibiotics-640x434.jpg" width="640" height="434">
  
    <figcaption class="caption">
	
			<div class="caption-credit">
							<a rel="nofollow" href="http://health.mo.gov/safety/antibioticresistance/generalinfo.php">Missouri Public Health</a>
				</div>
	  </figcaption>
  </figure>




    <p>Editing the sequence of bases in a DNA molecule is pretty straightforward in a test tube. Until recently, editing the DNA of a living organism had been a very large challenge, one that was more often avoided than taken up. But a system bacteria use to defeat viruses has been repurposed to make a versatile DNA editing system.</p>
<p>The system, called CRISPR-Cas9, takes short pieces of RNA as input. Any places it spots the same sequence in a DNA molecule, it makes a cut. Those cuts will then generally be repaired using any DNA sequence that roughly matches it. If researchers provide an edited version of the sequence, then the edits get incorporated into the cut DNA.</p>

<p>The system made news recently when it was used to edit the DNA of fertilized human embryos. But it's seen plenty of other uses, from <a href="http://arstechnica.com/science/2014/07/bacterial-antivirus-system-repurposed-to-attack-hiv-where-its-hiding/">targeting HIV infections</a> to creating a <a>mutagenic chain reaction</a> that can spread through populations of pests. Now, some researchers have turned it against another threat: antibiotic resistance.</p>
<p>It's not an obvious thing to target. Editing the DNA of bacteria involves getting DNA inside populations of bacterial cells in settings like hospitals. The most convenient way to do that is with a virus. And, if you're going to target bacteria with a virus, you might just as well use a virus that kills them, right?</p>
<p>Yes and no. Having a killer virus simply selects for bacteria that can resist infection, setting off an evolutionary arms race between the bugs and the virus. Meanwhile, the problem of antibiotic resistance is still with us.</p>
<p>So, some researchers at Tel Aviv's Sackler School of Medicine came up with a clever idea: why not create a virus that gives bacteria something that's useful to them, but gets rid of antibiotic resistance at the same time? Under normal growth circumstances, the bacteria would readily pick up the virus, because it's useful. But, when faced with an antibiotic assault, they'd be helpless to resist it.</p>
<p>To create this magical construct, the researchers turned to a virus that infects bacteria called ? (familiar to anyone who's taken a class in gene regulation). ? has a mode of infection in which it inserts itself into the host genome and resides there, dormant until some point in the future. ? was modified so that it would remain dormant indefinitely.</p>
<p>To give this version of ? something that's useful to bacteria, the authors equipped it with the CRISPR-Cas9 system along with genes for targeting RNAs that would direct it to other viruses. Now, if those viruses tried to infect a cell with the modified ? already in it, they'd be cut to pieces. In essence, they were using a virus to make bacteria immune to another virus. Viral infections went down by three orders of magnitude.</p>
<p>To make it useful to us, the researchers added a second set of genes for targeting RNAs. These directed the CRISPR-Cas9 system to cut up antibiotic resistance genes. This worked as expected: ? infected cells couldn't pick up the antibiotic resistance genes and, if they had any before the infection, they were lost. The bacteria remained susceptible to antibiotics.</p>
<p>So, in theory, wiping out antibiotic resistance in a hospital setting could be as simple as spraying the engineered ? virus, along with whatever virus it provides resistance to. In fact, if you engineered a virus that did several useful things for the bacteria, it could become possible for it to spread through wild populations via horizontal gene transfer.</p>
<p>That's the good news. The bad news is that it should be possible for evolution to modify the ? in a bacterial genome so it maintains its viral resistance abilities, but loses its ability to wipe out antibiotic resistance. In addition, ? only infects <em>E. coli</em>, and there are strains of that bacteria that are already resistant to it. Targeting other significant disease-causing bacteria will be a challenge.</p>
<p>Still, it's a creative approach that could pay dividends in some contexts. And it's possible that other researchers will figure out ways of extending the approach so it's more widely effective.</p>

<li class="thin-divide-bottom comment neutral read"
  data-post-score="9"
  data-post-negative="0"

    <span class="author" data-post-author="Hamilcar"><a href="http://arstechnica.com/civis/memberlist.php?mode=viewprofile&amp;u=364861&amp;sid=aa2774e503c5ad9f0f6a95c9914a774c">Hamilcar</a></span><span class="rank">Smack-Fu Master, in training</span>
      <aside class="icons">
        
          <a href="?comments=1&amp;post=29051759&amp;mode=quote#reply" class='quotelink icon' title="Reply">reply</a>
        
        <a href="http://arstechnica.com/science/2015/05/engineered-virus-protects-bacteria-while-eliminating-antibiotic-resistance/?comments=1&post=29051759#comment-29051759" title="2015-05-19T14:31:28-05:00" class="timeago datetime">Tue May 19, 2015 1:31 pm</a>

    <div class="body">
      It's important for people to understand that CRISPR/Cas is something like <span style="font-style: italic">a bacterial &quot;immune&quot; system</span>, which is <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1365-2958.2010.07326.x/full" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">already present in <span style="font-style: italic">E. Coli</span></a> (Wiley, probably pay-walled) and likely plays some role in phage/plasmid resistance.<br /><br />As a result, excessive concern over &quot;introducing&quot; CRISPR into <span style="font-style: italic">E. coli</span> is a bit misplaced. The endogenous CRISPR/Cas setup in <span style="font-style: italic">E. coli</span> is a bit weird, for sure:<br /><div class="quotetitle"><b>Quote:</b></div><div class="quotecontent">&quot;...neither insertion of new spacers nor interference with targeted invaders by non-manipulated CRISPR loci has been documented in Escherichia coli so far. Nevertheless, overexpression of the CRISPR2/CAS-E system of E. coli (associated to Cse-subtype CAS genes) located in recombinant plasmids, provides the cell with high levels of protection against phage infection, suggesting that the apparent inactivity of the genomic loci could be due to constrained expression.</div><br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic">E. coli</span> appears to regulate its endogenous CRISPR/Cas system using (surprise!) a histone-like particle:<br /><br /><div class="quotetitle"><b>Quote:</b></div><div class="quotecontent">Regulation of CRISPR2/CAS-E has been recently studied in E. coli K12 (Pul et al., 2010). This work brought to the arena an eminent player, the histone-like nucleoid structuring protein (H-NS), a well-known dual regulator (more often repressor) of gene expression in Gram-negative bacteria. This protein binds and represses promoters of both CRISPR2 arrays and casABCD12 operon, indicating that CRISPR-mediated resistance may only be conferred when silencing by H-NS is circumvented. In this issue of Molecular Microbiology, Pougach et al. (2010) and Westra et al. (2010) independently show that a high protection against ? is conferred by phage-matching spacers in hns-disrupted E. coli strains, demonstrating that derepression of CRISPR/CAS elements leads to efficient immunity (Fig. 1B). </div><br /><br />Why would <span style="font-style: italic">E. coli</span> want to so heavily repress its CRISPR system? Well, if you understand how CRISPR <a href="http://www.addgene.org/crispr/guide/" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">works</a>, you might be able to guess that the &quot;protospacer&quot; targets of CRISPR/Cas could accidentally include <span style="font-style: italic">endogenous bacterial targets</span>, leading to &quot;autoimmune&quot; targeting of the cells <span style="font-style: italic">own genes</span>, which would be extremely maladaptive, if not deadly.<br /><br />As a result (like with all things immunological), it is imperative that the bacteria keep their &quot;immune system&quot; on a very, very short leash. Depending on the idiosyncrasies of a particular bacterial niche, it may in fact be advantageous for species like <span style="font-style: italic">E. coli</span> to leave their CRISPR systems &quot;off&quot; by default. <br /><br />___<br />Likewise, concern about &quot;engineered viruses&quot; in the context of the lambda phage is a bit off the mark. Bacteriophage <span style="font-weight: bold">cannot</span> infect humans, period. Phage are very, very different from eukaryotic viruses.<br /><br />Indeed, according to some very recent, <span style="font-style: italic">very</span> intriguing findings by Dr. Jeremy Barr of the <a href="http://coralandphage.org/" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">Rowher group</a> (with assistance from the Doran and Wolkowicz groups), phage in the context of the human body may represent something like a form of <a href="http://www.pnas.org/content/110/26/10771.abstract" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">acquired immunity</a> in the human body, binding to branched mucins in the mucosa and helping to keep bacterial populations in check.<br /><br />Indeed, phage have been used <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phage_therapy" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">clinically</a> to treat bacterial infections in Georgia and Russia for many years. There are assuredly obstacles to the use of phage as treatments (Phage Pharmaceuticals is now defunct, for example), but it seems exceedingly likely that they will be able to play a substantial role in the management of bacterial infections <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3278648/" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">in the future</a>.<br /><br />edited it's its!
