		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">Tech sector tells Obama encryption backdoors “undermine human rights”</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Backdoors "could be exploited by even the most repressive or dangerous regimes."</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by     <a itemprop="url" href="http://arstechnica.com/author/david-kravets/" rel="author"><span itemprop="name">David Kravets</span></a>

	  </figcaption>
  </figure>




    <p>Technology giants like Apple, Google, and Microsoft urged President Barack Obama on Tuesday to refrain from supporting any US policy that would require the tech sector to install backdoors into their products so the authorities can access encrypted data.</p>
<p>In a <a href="//cdn.arstechnica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/cryptoletter.pdf">letter</a> (PDF) to Obama, dozens of tech companies, cryptologists, and rights groups said mandatory backdoors—which many authorities in the US government and abroad have been calling for—would weaken cybersecurity as well as "undermine human rights."</p>
<blockquote><p>More than undermining every American’s cybersecurity and the nation’s economic security, introducing new vulnerabilities to weaken encrypted products in the US would also undermine human rights and information security around the globe. If American companies maintain the ability to unlock their customers’ data and devices on request, governments other than the United States will demand the same access, and will also be emboldened to demand the same capability from their native companies. The US government, having made the same demands, will have little room to object. The result will be an information environment riddled with vulnerabilities that could be exploited by even the most repressive or dangerous regimes. That’s not a future that the American people or the people of the world deserve.</p></blockquote>
<p>Tuesday's letter comes as the White House is in the process of coming up with a position on the issue and in response to a chorus of government officials at home and abroad—including British Prime Minister David Cameron, FBI Director James Comey, and former Attorney General Eric Holder—<a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2015/01/uk-prime-minister-wants-backdoors-into-messaging-apps-or-hell-ban-them/">all calling for backdoors</a>.</p>
<p>Comey, for example, said last year that he was concerned about Apple producing phones that are encrypted by default, which likely precludes the authorities from accessing data on iPhones directly from a locked device's hardware, even with a warrant. The director said he was <a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2014/09/apple-google-default-cell-phone-encryption-concerns-fbi-director/">worried</a> about "companies marketing something expressly to allow people to place themselves beyond the law."</p>
<p>So far, there has been no congressional proposal to back up the calls from high-ranking government officials for backdoors. For the moment, the rhetoric appears to be a tactic to guilt the tech sector into putting backdoors into their products themselves.</p>
<p>Tuesday's letter, however, shows that the tech sector isn't caving—meaning legislation requiring backdoors would have to be passed. In the US, the law in question is the Communications Assistance for Law Enforcement Act of 1994, known as CALEA. The measure demands that telecommunication companies make their phone networks available to wiretaps. However, that requirement, at least for now, does not apply to phone hardware or most other communication services.</p>
<p>But that might change, especially when the White House announces, perhaps by this summer, a more formal position on the matter.</p>
<p>For the moment, Obama has hedged his position. The president has said he is a supporter and "believer in strong encryption" but also "<a href="http://arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2015/02/obama-hedges-position-on-encryption-its-good-its-bad/">sympathetic</a>" to law enforcement's need to prevent terror attacks.</p>
<p>The letter to Obama concludes with a question: "The Administration faces a critical choice: will it adopt policies that foster a global digital ecosystem that is more secure, or less?"</p>
<p>Kevin Bankston, the policy director of the New America Foundation's Open Technology Institute, organized the letter. Here's why, he <a href="https://www.newamerica.org/oti/massive-coalition-of-security-experts-tech-companies-and-privacy-advocates-presses-obama-to-oppose-surveillance-backdoors/">said</a>:</p>
<blockquote><p>Since last fall, the President has been letting his top intelligence and law enforcement officials criticize companies for making their devices more secure, and letting them suggest that Congress should pass anti-encryption, pro-backdoor legislation. That's despite unanimous consensus in the technical community thatbackdoors are bad for security, and despite lawmakers clearly signaling that they think it's a bad idea—most recently in a House oversight <a href="https://www.newamerica.org/oti/at-crypto-hearing-best-arguments-against-backdoor-mandates-come-from-members-of-congress-themselves/">hearing</a> where every lawmaker in attendance was critical of the government's position, one of them going so far as to call the idea of backdoors "<a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/blogs/the-switch/wp/2015/04/30/congressman-with-computer-science-degree-encryption-back-doors-are-technologically-stupid/">technologically stupid</a>."</p>
<p>We decided it was time for the Internet community—industry, advocates, and experts—to draw a line in the sand. We're calling on Obama to put an end to these dangerous suggestions that we should deliberately weaken the cybersecurity of American products and services.</p></blockquote>

What changed from iOS 3 to iOS 8 (and inbetween) is which data was protected by a encryption key that was destroyed when the phone was locked. It was vastly expanded in iOS 8. <br /><br />You can see these different methods if you look at <a href="https://developer.apple.com/library/prerelease/ios/documentation/Cocoa/Reference/Foundation/Classes/NSFileManager_Class/index.html#//apple_ref/doc/constant_group/File_Protection_Values" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">NSFileProtection</a> available since iOS 4 to change the default encryption key retention method on a file-by-file basis. In iOS 8, this changed so that the keys necessary to decrypt mail attachments were destroyed when the phone was locked and would remain encrypted until the phone was unlocked.<br /><br />This was true of various other data types as well. If the phone had been unlocked even once after boot, those data types were available. <span style="font-weight: bold">This caused some issues if someone found a security vulnerability in the lock screen (which happened often)</span> or if malware exploited a different vulnerability or if someone could connect a computer or other CPU to the iOS device, which allowed them to directly read memory (this latter issue was fixed with iOS 7 and &quot;trusted device&quot; support).<br /><br />However, there was another serious vulnerability. The A4 SoC used in the iPhone 4 and below had a serious security issue in the silicon. Which meant it was possible to bypass the passcode even if the device was locked if an attacker had physical access to the device. No amount of changes to the iOS software could fix this vulnerability. This prevented Apple from making a blanket statement about iPhone security. Since iOS 8 only runs on the A5 or later, Apple can now issue a blanket statement about iPhone security with iOS 8 installed.<br /><br />Edit: To clarify, Apple's statements about iOS security during the iOS 8 announcement were more of a PR move than an actual policy or technical change. To ease people's worries about potential backdoors, especially as Apple was expanding into more markets that had absolutely no trust of the U.S. government. Also to counter people that repeatedly declare &quot;iOS is the least secure mobile OS&quot;.

In matters of intelligence &amp; national security, Government insists that it's status demands privileged oversight of any matter, situation, circumstance or detail that otherwise merits confidential consideration. <span style="font-style: italic"><span style="text-decoration: underline">Yet this very claim to the status of ultimate arbiter also, somehow, neuters Governments ultimate obligation to transparency &amp; accountability</span>.</span><br /><br />3.) Justification for all law enforcement, national security &amp; intelligence is fanciful at best &amp; farcical at worst: <span style="font-style: italic"><span style="text-decoration: underline">We're from the government &amp; can be trusted because we're the good guys &amp; are here to help</span>!</span><br /><br />And any meaningful discussion or debate pretty much ends right there. Having drawn authorization &amp; agency from The People, Government therefore has no need, obligation or responsibility to maintain it's preordained &amp; fundamental accountability to those same people.<br /><br />Just Because.</div><br />On the otherhand, complete transparency can in certain cases jeopardize the safety and security of the nation against enemies both foreign and domestic. It's a fine balancing act between ensuring domestic tranquility and providing for the common defense, both of which are top priorities as stated in the Constitution.</div><br />Granted, it's a herculean task &amp; one whose outcomes are <span style="font-weight: bold"><span style="font-style: italic"><span style="text-decoration: underline">guaranteed to be less than ideal</span>!</span></span> <br /><br />But until representatives of government are willing to acknowledge the functional imperfections inherent to any given circumstance they operate in &amp; then be willing to proceed in a demonstrably good faith effort to mitigate for these imperfections &amp; inefficiencies, my response to their flippant justification of <span style="font-style: italic">&quot;Just Because!&quot;</span> is an equally flippant <span style="font-style: italic">&quot;Just No! (Unless of course you want to talk, like adults &amp; equals).&quot;</span><br /><br />Given the illegalities of government agencies that have been uncovered &amp; disclosed, it defies reason that any government request or &quot;demand&quot; should include an assumption of trust in government integrity &amp; good intent.
      
    </div>
