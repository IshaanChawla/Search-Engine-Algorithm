<p>Isn’t it always the way? You plan a vacation and yet somehow work finds you. But it turns out this isn't such a bad thing. Cars Technica happens to be in Santa Monica for a few days, but as luck would have it, Volvo is also in town.</p>
<p>The company is getting ready to demo its new XC90 SUV to the press early next week. Although we aren’t able to stay long enough to take part, Jim Nichols at Volvo was kind enough to spend an hour with us, showing off some of the vehicle’s new features including the latest version of Volvo’s infotainment system, Sensus Connect.</p>
<p>The XC90 is Volvo’s new range-topping SUV, and we got a brief drive in both the T6 and T8 models. The T6 features a turbo- and supercharged 4 cylinder engine (316 horsepower, 295 lb-ft); the T8 is a twin-engined plug-in hybrid good for 400 horsepower (313 hp from the 4 cylinder gas engine and another 87 hp from the electric motor). As one would expect from a Volvo, the XC90s are packed full of the latest and greatest safety equipment, in line with the company’s ambitious "<a href="http://web.origin.volvocars.com/intl/top/about_volvo/corporate/volvo-sustainability/safety/pages/vision-2020.aspx">Vision 2020</a>" plan. Th idea is that by the year 2020, no-one should be killed or seriously injured in a new Volvo. Even with only a brief stint behind the wheel, it was long enough to put Volvo’s lane assist technology to the test on the slow-moving I-10 freeway (the system only works up to 30 mph currently). It’s rather unnerving to take one’s hands off the wheel on a busy freeway, but as you can see from the video, it works.</p>
<figure class="video center full" style="width:100%"><iframe src="https://vine.co/v/eKUMEuaXOqA/embed/simple" scrolling="no" width="100%" height="480" frameborder="0"></iframe><figcaption class="caption"><div class="caption-text">Look Ma, no hands! Via <a href="https://vine.co/u/908586613171433472">Elle Cayabyab Gitlin</a></div> </figcaption></figure>
<p>A full review of the XC90 will have to wait for later in the year once the vehicles have arrived on Volvo’s press fleet. (At Cars Technica, we like to spend at least a week or so with a vehicle on our terms in order to review it properly.) In the meantime, we can certainly share a few of the XC90 sights.</p>
<p>The XC90 T6 pricing starts at $48,900 and is on sale now, and will be joined by the hybrid T8 later this year, starting at $68,100.</p>

      <div class="quotetitle"><a href="http://arstechnica.com/civis/viewtopic.php?p=29039387#p29039387" class="postlink" rel="nofollow">kdemello1980</a> wrote:</div><div class="quotecontent"><br /> Touch screen controls require one to take their eyes off the road to operate, and if the glare is so bad in these photos, imagine what it would be like in the sun.<br /><br /> The driver assist should come in handy when the driver is forced to take both hands off the wheel to operate the climate controls (one hand to touch the screen and another to block out the sunlight).<br /></div><br /><br /> Pardon my rant on new cars with technology for the sake of technology. <br /><br /> I can't agree more with the above quote. I've never understood why people want touchscreens in cars. Or digital gauges that are just a screen too. I love my (now aging) BMW 's I-drive system. It's the first generation with the non-hd screen and a single large knob for control near where one would rest their hand anyway. The menus are almost never more than one level deep, very simple, and can be operated without looking after one day with the car. It's a low res screen but interesting in that when hit with direct light (which is rare thanks to the screen residing in a little cave) the image on screen is still completely visible,  albeit the colors appear inverted like a film negative. I wonder what type of panel it is? Probably no good for HD res anyway. BMW did their homework on the ergonomics and end user experience though. This is one thing I love about BMW, at least older ones before they started catering to the creature comfort, distraction obsessed, general American population. I'm clearly not as impressed with newer ones. <br /><br /> I've spent some time in brand new cars (time will tell if this Volvo falls into this category) with horrible ergonomics. They feel as though the design and marketing teams had a wall covered in techno-babble and features, threw some darts at it blindfolded and said, &quot;well, we have to cram all of that in there, figure out how to make it fit for $35k&quot;.<br /> <br />Like my mother's 2015 Accord which she got so decked out it makes its Acuras brethren look cheap (she doesn't even know what Bluetooth is, thank you crooked dealership). It has every option possible. Touch screens everywhere. Can't see any of them in direct light. Distracting &quot;safety&quot;  features like a lane departure system that beeps when you're within 10 meters of anything resembling a line, some sort of light system that shines lights at the driver warning you when you're too close to objects (very distracting, I know I'm close to another car, I'm parallel parking!), the mirrors are curved to avoid blindspots but are really just useless and cameras that awkwardly show the lane next to you in a horrible fish-eye distortion while driving, its absurdly disorienting. Besides, they already put curved mirrors on the car. The back up camera is horribly low res displayed on an equally low res screen. The camera placement was genius too. On cool mornings the warm exhaust completely fogs over on the lens. There are two screens that make up the infotainment system that appear to be completely redundant, at some times they literally display the same information. Additionally, she has trouble with working the screens while driving, especially because I believe they're resistive touch screens which are notorious for not registering touches correctly. I'm amazed the car didn't come with a STYLUS! Because, Technology!<br /><br />I'm not trying to rail against genuine safety features, just poorly implemented ones. Cars that can brake on their own? I wish we weren't so distracted and didn't need them but lets be honest, people need them. Back up cameras? I don't use them beside a cursory glance before I turn around. They're all 0.7MP fish-eye cameras anyways, how about an 8MP true wide angle lens, they were in smartphones circa 2010. We're talking about a $30K+ hunk of machinery, we should get what we pay for not bottom of the barrel screens, $0.50 camera's, half-assed safety features that serve to distract more than protect and infotainment systems that require said safety features to save you while you navigate the touch screens. Touchscreens, keep them the hell out of cars. This is the one thing that bothers me about Tesla's model S, one, big, touch, screen. I was looking forward to the model 3, we'll see if they continue the touchscreen totalitarianism in the more affordable model. I'll probably still get one and learn to live with it. <br /><br />*End rant on poorly implemented technology in cars*<br /><br />I hope Volvo, being so safety-centric, can see passed peoples desires for more screens, cameras everywhere, connected this and that, and actually implement all this technology in a safe, useful way. I'm not keen on the looks but I'm sure they've done their homework, some people will like it and buy it. I like how the automotive companies are slowly, ever so slowly shifting to smaller and smaller combustion engines and heftier electric drive systems. If they do it slowly enough maybe people won't notice! I'm curious about real world fuel efficiency compared to the weight and size of the vehicle. The no hands bit is interesting. Most cars with power steering should simply track straight without any input... nonetheless it may be a useful safety feature when fully developed. So now cars steer and brake themselves, now if only we could get them to man the accelerator full time as well, people are notoriously bad with that pedal in particular. I hope that crystal shifter doesn't cause problems with refracting sunlight into peoples eyes, there are just some things that are best left out of cars, like you know, prisms. The gauge cluster looks alright for an LCD display. Some try to cram way too much in them because everything needs to be entertaining and futuristic. I still prefer real gauges but it is 2015.
