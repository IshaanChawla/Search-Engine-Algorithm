		<h1 class="heading" itemprop="headline">YouTube Kids app has sex, drugs, and program-length ads, advocacy groups say</h1>
		<h2 class="standalone-deck" itemprop="description">Consumer groups complain&mdash;again&mdash;to the FTC about Google's latest app for kids.</h2>
    		<div class="post-meta">
      
  <p class="byline" itemprop="author creator" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
  by     <a itemprop="url" href="http://arstechnica.com/author/megan-geuss/" rel="author"><span itemprop="name">Megan Geuss</span></a>

  </p>

			<div class="mobile-meta-controls">

	<section id="article-guts">
		<div itemprop="articleBody" class="article-content clearfix">
    



    <p>Child and consumer advocacy groups are unhappy with Google's new YouTube Kids app, which launched in February of this year. They say <a href="https://vimeo.com/127837914">the app contains videos</a> like a <em>Sesame Street</em> episode “dubbed with long strings of expletives,” references to pedophilia in a <em>My Little Pony</em> review, and a Budweiser commercial.</p>
<p>Other videos are less insidious—how-tos on using circular saws, ASMR videos showing a woman lighting matches, and TED talks for more mature audiences—but given that the YouTube Kids app description says that it's “Made for Ages 5 and Under,” the videos are out of place on the app, the group says.</p>
<p>On Tuesday, the Campaign for a Commercial-Free Childhood (CCFC) and the Center for Digital Democracy sent a letter <a href="https://www.change.org/p/google-inc-demand-google-recall-youtube-kids-app-due-to-content-unsuitable-for-children">and a video</a> to the Federal Trade Commission, documenting a search that the groups did in the YouTube Kids app on May 5. The letter is a follow-up to an FTC complaint that the CCFC <a href="http://www.commercialfreechildhood.org/youtubekids">filed back in April</a> alleging that the YouTube Kids app mixes branded channels from companies like McDonald's, Mattel, and Fisher-Price too freely with real children's programming. The app's features “take advantage of children’s developmental vulnerabilities and violate long-standing media and advertising safeguards that protect children viewing television,” the CCFC wrote in April.</p>
<p>Google has not responded to Ars' request for comment. According to <a href="http://blogs.wsj.com/digits/2015/05/19/googles-youtube-kids-app-criticized-for-inappropriate-content/?mod=rss_Technology"><em>The Wall Street Journal</em></a>, “Content for the app is picked using a combination of automatic filters, user feedback and manual reviews.” The <em>Journal</em> also notes that the app has over 1,000 reviews and a four-star rating in the Google Play store, but <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.google.android.apps.youtube.kids&amp;hl=en">several reviewers say</a> that the app needs better filters.</p>
<p>Parents can flag videos they think are inappropriate and Google will review them for possible removal. “YouTube handles tremendous breadth, depth and scale of content,” the <a href="https://support.google.com/youtubekids/answer/6162907">support section of the YouTube Kids app</a> says. “... [W]hile we work hard to get it right, it’s nearly impossible to have 100% accuracy. That’s why we give parents the option to turn off search for a more restricted experience.”</p>
